The modifying pattern stabilizes the pigmentation reaction



On the shell shown below, in addition to the white drop-like pattern elements, regions with coherent background pigmentation are visible that show darker lines perpendicular to the growing edge. This indicates the formation of an additional stable pattern. However, small white drops  interrupt the dark lines. This suggests that the elevated pigment production leads to the preferential breakdown of the pigment production. This interplay of amplification and breakdown is the central issue of this chapter (and still an open problem).

Stable perpendicular stripes will occur if a second stable pattern forming system exists that, for example, locally increases the substrate production for the pigmentation reaction. In the upper part of the following simulation, the stable pattern is shown green. In the lower part, only the enhanced pigmentation is shown.

Thus, the assumption is that an additional pattern exists that stabilizes the pigment pattern. It depends on the pigmentation reaction. In the case of the perpendicular stripes shown above, both pattern are stable. On this and the subsequent pages it is shown that such a reaction can generate in other ranges of parameters a surprising diversity of patterns that have their counterparts on the real shells.

On Conus pennaceus BORN, branches are the dominating pattern elements. Branching will occur it the trigger of the enhancing pattern (green in the simulation further below) shifts the system from a pulse-like to a steady state activation. Cells remain activated sufficiently long. If the refractory period of adjacent cells is over backwards waves can be triggered.

The figure below shows another region of the same shell given at the top of this page. It displays a remarkable feature for which no satisfactory model is yet available. The 'staggered wine glass pattern' result from waves that move toward each other with increasing velocity. However, instead of annihilating each other, they give rise to a local activation that remains stable for a certain time, forming the 'stem' of the wine glass pattern. Later, this activation broadens, split and start to move to both sides, giving rise to the next accelerating waves.
