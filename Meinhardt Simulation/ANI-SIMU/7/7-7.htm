Open problems: the relation of enhancement and suppression –
one ore two modifying reactions?

As mentioned above, many of these shell patterns suggest a simultaneous involvement of an enhancing and an extinguishing modification. For example, the perpendicular dark lines on Conus episcopus shown below arguing for an enhancement, and the white droplet-like patterns along these dark lines arguing for a destabilization under the enhancing influence. The large-scale breakdowns of pigmentation suggest a rapid spread of an extinguishing reaction while the existence of branch formation along narrow lines suggest that the pigmentation reaction changes  from time to time from a wave-like spread into a temporary steady state that allows the trigger of backward waves. Interestingly on the shell below are also unsuccessful attempts to initiate a backwards wave, leading to small triangles attached to the oblique lines. A satisfying coherent explanation is still missing.

Of course, one could imagine that both an enhancing and an extinguishing reaction are superimposed. An example is given in the simulation below. In the dark region the even the darker lines result from the enhancing reaction (green). In the regions that are not in the steady state, this leads to branch formation. In turn, the enhanced pigment reaction leads more rapidly to the trigger of the extinguishing reaction (red), causing the preferential dropouts of the pigmentation along the dark lines. The finer dark lines are simulated by an extinguishing reaction that follows the pigmentation reaction in a wave-like manner.

The preferential formation of white regions in the center of the shell is simulated by an increased production rate of the substrate for the  extinguishing reaction (plotted at the top of the simulation). Although this simulation requires the interaction of 6 substances, the similarity between the natural and the simulated pattern is not overwhelming. The simulation below is an attempt to combine enhancement and extinguishing reaction. An analogy to doping in sport was used to illustrate the basic idea. Doping releases the available resources of the body, enabling temporarily a higher output of power. This, however, can cause a breakdown when resources become exhausted. Without doping, a continuous output of power is possible over a longer period, although at a lower level. With doping, more resources are removed than can be replenished. Therefore, exhaustion comes into effect only if the system is without doping at the border where a continuous activation is just possible. In contrast, if the ability to replenish resources is sufficiently high, no enhancement-induced breakdown will take place. In the simulation below, the breakdown is initiated in the regions of the darker lines as required for the pattern on Conus pennaceus. Again, the match between the natural and the simulated pattern is limited. A more appropriate interaction waits to be discovered.
