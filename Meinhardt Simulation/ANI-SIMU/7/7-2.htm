The modifying pattern extinguishes the pigmentation reaction
 
 

The white drop-like pattern on Conus marmoreus results from a relatively slow spread of pigmentation while a termination of pigment deposition occurs almost simultaneously over a large region. It is, so to speak, a collective breakdown. In this photograph, the regions at the growing edge in which pigment were produced at the end of life of the animal is clearly visible.

According to the model a bistable pigment-producing system (black) exists. The activated state spreads slowly by infection. After a certain time delay the pigment reaction triggers a second and much faster spreading reaction (red, invisible on the shell) that extinguishes the pigmentation reaction. The animated simulation and the space-time plot below show the same simulation. The extinguishing pattern is assumed to degrade the activator more rapidly. Therefore, it shifts the system from a steady state mode into an oscillating mode. The activation of the pigment-producing system is maintained at the margins of a pigmented region since there the substrate concentration is sufficiently high. After the short extinguishing reaction is over, the spread of pigmentation and its maintenance in recently activated regions continues. Thus, the pattern results from a permanent change between a slowly  spreading steady state and a rapid large-scale extinguishing with survival of the activation at front of the spreading pigment reaction:
 


In a related species, the extinguishing reaction is obviously stronger. This leads in many cases to a termination of the pigmentation. Those sites that were recently activated have a lower chance of  survival – a feature that we will meet again with oblique chains of  triangles.
 



 
 




The shell shown below shows a modulation of the pigmentation. The regions of high pigmentation consist in fact of densely packed horizontal lines. Either the steady state of the pigment reaction is close to a transition into an oscillating mode. Alternatively, as shown below right, if the extinguishing reaction occurs more frequently but is less effective, the pigment system frequently escapes from down-regulation. The rapid spread of the extinguishing reaction leads to a partial synchronization of the extinguishing reaction. The result is a modulation of the pigmented region by parallel lines.


There is a non-trivial discrepancy between the natural pattern and the simulations. The "white drops" have relative sharp tips in the simulations while they are more rounded in the natural pattern (see also the first shell on this page).
