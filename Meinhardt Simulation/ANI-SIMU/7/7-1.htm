Generation of complex patterns by the superposition of several pattern forming systems

Many shells show patterns far more complex than those simulated so far. In chapter 4 patterns were discussed that result from the modification of the pigment-producing system by a second, not directly visible pattern. In these cases, the modifying pattern was stable. This is, however, only a special case since a change in a single time constant can be sufficient to switch from a stable into an oscillating mode. Therefore, it is assumed that the complex patterns described in this and the following two chapters result from a pigment producing system that is modified by one or more time-dependent, usually invisible, patterns.

Although a huge diversity of patterns exists, a closer inspection reveals that the patterns share many common elements. In the following series, each subsequent shell pattern has common elements with the preceding one but also displays new features:

    

Conus marmoreus (above left) shows white drop-like regions on a dark pigmented background. In Conus marchionatus the white drops are enlarged at the expense of the pigmented regions. The pattern is reminiscent of staggered wine glasses. Conus pennaceus shows, in addition, regions with a coherent background pigmentation that is structured by dark perpendicular lines that are occasionally interrupted by small white drops.

    

In Conus auratus (above left), the dark lines are maintained without the white drops.  Instead, a periodic large-scale transition to oblique lines with crossings occurs. Shortly before such a transition occurs, the continuous background becomes modulated parallel to the growing edge (arrow). Such axially oriented parallel lines on top of a pigmented background are a characteristic pattern element on Conus textile. Non-pigmented regions with a drop-like shape occasionally appear. Their lower borders are formed by dark pigmented lines. In regions without pigmented background the pattern displays fine lines with a mesh-like arrangement as mentioned above. Similar parallel lines with occasional loops (tongues) are characteristic for Clithon or Neritina oualaniensis (right). Here, however, the regions with fine lines are missing.

    

In the specimen of Clithon above left the narrow spaced parallel lines are framed by oblique lines, causing the overall impression of connected triangles. This pattern is also characteristic of the bivalved mussel Lioconcha castrensis. Similarly, in the shell of the snail Voluta vespertilio oblique lines and triangles result from very fine parallel lines. The larger triangles that occasionally form may become the starting points for branches.
