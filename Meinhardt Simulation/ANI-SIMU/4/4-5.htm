Diffusion can lead to coexistence of oscillating and stable patterns

The pattern Voluta musica is characterized by two bundles of fine lines perpendicular to the growing edge, interspersed by lines parallel to the growing edge as well as by isolated patches. This indicates that oscillating and stable patterns coexist.


The condition for oscillating patterns are that either the inhibitor molecules have a longer half-life than the activator molecules or that the rate of substrate production is higher than the decay rate of the activator molecules. It is easy to see that a diffusion of either the inhibitor or of the substrate can cause stabilization. The diffusion of the inhibitor away from a local maximum is equivalent to a shorter half life. Whether an inhibitor molecule decayed or was removed by diffusion does not matter for a cell. Thus, diffusion contributes to the tendency to switch into a stable pattern. Likewise, substrate obtained from neighboring cells increases the available substrate and thus contributes also to a transition into a stable state. This, however, this can only work if a concentration difference exist between adjacent cells otherwise the same number of molecules would be obtained and lost from neighboring cells and diffusion would be without effect. Thus, in addition to the diffusion of the antagonist, a certain phase difference is required between adjacent cells.

In the simulation above left, the diffusion of the substrate is high  enough that some cells enter into a stable activation, causing the long perpendicular lines. In-between these regions, the oscillations are still going on. It is clearly visible that the local transitions into stable activations occur only after the lines obtained a certain steepness, i.e., after a certain phase difference between cells accumulated.
