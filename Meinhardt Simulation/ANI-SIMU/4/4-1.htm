Simulations for chapter 4 :Superposition of stable and periodic patterns

.

The oscillation frequency is space-dependent: wavy lines.
 
 

The pattern on Natica euzona results from oscillations that have different frequencies along the growing edge. In some regions, pigment deposition occurs in rapid succession, in others with longer intervals. Obviously, at least two pattern forming systems are superimposed. One pattern is essentially stable in time. It is not visible and becomes evident only due to the space-dependent modulation of oscillating pigment deposition.

In the model it is assumed that the substrate production (bb in equation 2.4, curve at top right) is not uniform. A higher production rate leads to a higher oscillation frequency. Therefore, lines of pigmentation are formed with different spacing. In the transition zone lines can merge or end blindly.

If initially all cells are in the same phase, subsequent pigment production will occur earlier in the cells having a higher substrate production. In the absence of any activator diffusion, the phase difference between neighboring cells will increase with each further oscillation until any phase relation is lost. This is especially obvious if some random fluctuations are assumed as in the figure below left. Diffusion of the activator has the tendency to synchronize neighboring cells since cells activated earlier advance their delayed neighbors by activator exchange (center). Some substrate diffusion is required to obtain a good correspondence with the natural pattern (right). It enables a slowing down and merging of waves.

To model the narrower bands in the region of high oscillation frequencies, the decay rate of the activator (ra) and the source density s are assumed to have the same spatial pattern as the substrate production rate bb.
