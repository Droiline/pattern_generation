Blindly ending lines: wave termination

Cells in regions of high substrate supply act as pacemakers. After a traveling wave enters a region of low substrate production, the substrate concentration (green) may be too low for the wave to survive. On the shell this leads to a blindly ending line. The pattern on Amoria undulata shows this behavior:



A particular wave will become slower as it enters a region of lower substrate production but it may find sufficient substrate to survive. However, due to this reduced speed, the subsequent wave may terminate since less time is available for the substrate concentration to recover. Thus, wave termination is expected to occur in regions where neighboring cells have different oscillation frequencies. As can be seen from the natural pattern above right, at the position of a terminating line the subsequent line shows a characteristic irregularity: a deformation towards the terminated line. In terms of the model this has the following reason: at positions left untouched by the terminating wave, the substrate was not consumed for a longer period and the concentration remained at a higher level. The subsequent wave has therefore more 'fuel to burn'. In addition, a slight diffusion of the substrate can maintain a low level of activation until sufficient substrate becomes available again (see below).

The following animated simulation shows the situation along the growing edge. One wave is just able to survive, the next terminates:



Wave termination is also clearly visible in the shell shown below. On this shell, a general gradient exists in the excitability of the system. It is highest at the left side. Therefore, the left side act as pace maker and the speed of the waves decline on their way, resulting in the bent lines. Again, some waves terminate as they enter a less excitable region. The subsequent lines show the advanced trigger. The modulation of the background pigmentation indicates the orientation of the growing edge in the course of time.

Role of antagonist (substrate) diffusion in the survival of waves

The shell of Natica undulata (below right) shows thick lines that are occasionally connected by thin lines. This pattern results from a merging of two waves. The same phenomenon is visible on the shell of Natica euzona. This is non-trivial since usually one wave cannot catch up a preceding wave. Such a pattern is possible, however,  if the antagonist shows a moderate degree of diffusion. Then,  in a situation when otherwise wave termination would occur, activation can be maintained at a low level due to the surplus of substrate obtained from the surrounding cells. Very thin and steep lines are indicative for such a near-arrest of waves without termination. The substrate diffusion has to be so low that wave formation is not abolished by lateral inhibition. On some shells only the beginning and the end of such a line is visible (white arrowheads in the figure below). The renewed trigger of activation indicates that a low-level activation was there even if it was insufficient to evoke a visible trace of pigmentation. The simulations at right show the influence of diffusion. The same initial situation is assumed; the central figure is without, the right with diffusion. The regions with a low density of black pixels are regions of low-level activation.




Diffusion plays also a crucial role in the merging of waves in the Nautilus shell. In Voluta musica, the stabilization leads even to transitions into permanent activations.
