Steep lines and the saturation of autocatalysis

The pattern on the shell Cypraea diluculum shows a mixture of more or less parallel  thick lines and very thin steep lines:

Thick lines indicate that the phase of pigment production is a substantial fraction of the total cycle. This will occurs if the autocatalysis saturates: If  the maximum activator concentration has an upper bound, the consumption of the available substrate (or the production of sufficient inhibitor) takes longer. In regions of lower substrate production, the oscillations frequencies are lower. Consequently, the regions with the thick lines act as pacemakers. The regions of lower oscillation frequencies are over-tacted by the regions of high frequencies. These cells do not reach their fully excitable state since the next trigger occurs to early. Therefore, the spread of the wave is slow and the lines are steep. The following simulation shows the situation at the growing edge. Both right and left marginal sides are regions of high substrate production (substrate is shown in green):


Waves trigger only in the region of high substrate production and spread rapidly within these regions. In contrast, the activation spreads only slowly in the region of lower substrate production. The assumption of saturating autocatalysis provides a rationale why thick parallel and thin steep lines coexists. The saturation leads to a reduced maximum concentration. Therefore, it takes longer to infect neighboring cells. On the other hand, since the activation lasts longer, more time is available to infect the next cell. Therefore, waves terminate only rarely and a substantial phase difference can accumulate between neighboring regions. This leads to the steep lines.
 
 

The pattern on the specimen above provides strong support for the stipulation that the steep lines result from an accumulating phase difference. Obviously, a global perturbation took place for the animal, leading to an interruption of the steep lines on both sides of the central zone. The pigmentation reappears in that region more or less simultaneously. Only in the course of time the phase difference will accumulate again. In the simulation at right, the perturbation is implemented by assuming a sudden reduction to 10% of the actual activator concentration in each cell.
