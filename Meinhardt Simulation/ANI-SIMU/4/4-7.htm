The stable pattern inhibits the oscillating pattern

In normal pattern forming systems, the activated regions are usually smaller than the non-activated regions. Therefore, narrow non-pigmented bands suggest that small activated areas exists in which pigmentation is suppressed. An example is the pattern on Austrocochlea adelaidae.

On the shell shown above, not only the spatial gaps between the pigmentation lines are small. The duration of the activation is much longer than the intervals between the activations. As mentioned above, the duration of activation can be elongated by saturation, but not to this extend. In the simulation above a separate extinguishing pattern (red) is assumed. In some regions it remains in a steady state, suppressing permanently any pigmentation there. In between, burst-like activations of the extinguishing pattern do occur, causing abrupt suppression of pigment deposition. After this short pulse, the pigment reaction returns rapidly to a steady state activation. Therefore, a constant pigmentation over a long period with short interruptions is presumably generated by pigment system in a stable steady state combined with a separate extinguishing reaction. The simulation below shows the situation at the growing edge.

The assumption of an extinguishing pattern is supported by the pattern of Conus ebraeus. The non-pigmented regions form a reversed fishbone pattern. As in the case described above, regions in which the extinguishing pattern is permanently activated remain free of pigmentation. Traveling waves of the extinguishing reaction form the white oblique lines.
