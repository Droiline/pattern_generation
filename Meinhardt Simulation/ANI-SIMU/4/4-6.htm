Fishbone-like patterns: stable source regions of traveling waves

On some shells, the pigment producing reaction remain in particular regions in a steady state, while oscillations and traveling waves take place in the remaining regions.

The pattern on Cypraea ziczac suggests that waves spread from these steady state regions, forming the oblique lines. As usual, annihilations during collisions lead to the typical V-like pattern elements.  In the animated simulation shown below, the distributions at the growing edge are shown:




In the simulation above, the localized steady states are obtained by assuming a position-dependent substrate production. Later this local stabilization will  be treated by assuming two each other stabilizing systems, a mode that can be deduced from many patterns that look overtly very different.

Both in the fishbone-like pattern discussed on this page and in the Voluta musica pattern on the preceding page, stripes perpendicular to the growing edge are a characteristic feature. However, only in the fishbone pattern shown above traveling waves are initiated at these stable activations. There, the steady state is maintained due to the action of the modifying pattern. In contrast, the stable activations in the musica-type patterns result from a diffusion-based stabilization. Thus, the permanently activated cells are surrounded by zones of inhibition, preventing any spread of waves as in the fishbone pattern.

The shell shown below also displays two narrow stable stripes (a closer inspection revels that these  consist in fact of narrow-spaced dots). In contrast to the fishbone-pattern discussed above, these stripes seems to have only a limited and indifferent influence on the oscillating system. Waves can start there, end there or pass through.
