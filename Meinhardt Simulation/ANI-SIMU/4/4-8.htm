The stable pattern can be still be active

The shell of Scaphella junonia displays rows of patches perpendicular to the growing edge. At some regions, new rows of dots are inserted while at others large patches split into two. The latter feature suggests that new maxima of a stable patterning systems become inserted that suppresses pigment formation.

In the simulation above, a stable activator (blue) - inhibitor system is assumed that suppresses the oscillations of a second system (black). Thus, oscillations can occur only in the space between the suppressing maxima. If the distance between two maxima of the suppressing reaction becomes too large, a new  maximum can appear between the two. This leads to the split of row with large patches into two rows with smaller patches. At least initially the separated patches maintain synchrony.

Oscillations may only occur if the space between the suppressing stable maxima is sufficiently large. If it is not, a relatively broad white stripe results. During growth, the distance between two inhibiting maxima can become large enough such that oscillation can set in. A new row of dots becomes established in a region that formed earlier a larger non-pigmented gap. As shown in the natural pattern above, the oscillation frequency can be initially lower. Such a small patch may appear initially in synchrony only with every second normal-sized neighboring patch.
