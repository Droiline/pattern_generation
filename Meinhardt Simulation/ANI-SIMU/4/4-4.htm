Nautilus: different oscillation frequencies correlate with different growth rates

To get the coiled shell shape of Nautilus pompilius, the growth rate must be higher at the outer periphery than at inner regions. Similarly, the number of pigmentation lines at the shell periphery is approximately double the number in the inner region, indicating a gradient in the oscillation frequency.

In the simulation an activator-substrate reaction (equation 2.4) with a (linearly) graded substrate production bb(x) is assumed. The same gradient may also be used to maintain the higher growth rate on the periphery required for the spiral shape of the shell.

Some stripes on the Nautilus shell show bifurcations. This pattern results from a merging of two waves. As discussed above, this is a non-trivial behavior since it requires that a second wave catches up a first.  This is possible if the antagonist shows some diffusion.  In the simulation above the substrate diffuses slowly, in the simulation below the substrate does not diffuse.
