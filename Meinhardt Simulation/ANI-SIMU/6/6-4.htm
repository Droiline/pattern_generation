Open questions


Although many features are correctly reproduced, several problems still remain. On the shell shown below, for instance, a transition into a steady state occurs that shows modulations parallel to the growing edge. In the model, transitions into a steady state with horizontal modulations also occur (for equation and constants click here). However, from the model it would be expected that the modulation becomes more pronounced shortly before the transition from the steady state into pulse-like activation occurs, in contrast to the natural patterns. The reason for the expectation is as follows: during triangle formation the hormone concentration increases due to the growing number of pigment-producing cells. This leads to a gradual change from the stable steady state towards the oscillatory mode. As shown in the simulation at right, it is expected that the horizontal modulation becomes strongest shortly before the large-scale breakdown occurs. In contrast, in the natural pattern this transition occurs abrupt and without any announcement.

    


Further, in the simulations global transitions occur from the normal pattern towards dense oblique lines that don't branch. After some time, the system return to normal pattern formation. The reason for these global transitions is unclear. Long oblique lines with relatively little branching is visible on the shell in the center. In the shell at left, a particular region seems to maintain a steady state  (perpendicular line) from which waves move to both sides. A similar phenomenon can be seen in the simulation.


Many Oliva shells show a similar but seemingly independent pattern in the background. It is unclear whether this patterning results from an independent system or from a sequential patterning that uses the same system.

The background pattern is also clearly visible on  the following  shell. This shell also demonstrates, very convincingly, that branch formation is globally controlled. Once a high density of waves is achieved, branch formation becomes unlikely and most waves terminate simultaneously upon collision.
