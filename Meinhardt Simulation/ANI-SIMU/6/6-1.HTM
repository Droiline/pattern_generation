Branch initiation by global control




Several patterns suggest that global control mechanisms play a role. In the shell shown below, termination of pigment production occurs simultaneously at very distant positions.

On other shells, this global control is more hidden. The shell of Oliva porphyria shows oblique lines with branching. A branch along an oblique line indicates the sudden formation of a backward wave. As shown in the enlargement, at a particular moment many backwards-running waves are initiated simultaneously at distant locations.

   

For traveling waves in excitable media the sudden formation of backward waves is a very unusual event. The new waves must spread into a region that should be refractory due to the primary wave. Therefore, a spontaneously formed backwards running wave should never occur.

As mentioned above, normal traveling waves in excitable media annihilate each other upon a collision. This would lead to a continuos decline of the number of traveling waves until no wave is left. (below left). Branch formation leads to a doubling of the number of traveling waves. Thus, the initiation of backwards running waves would be one way to maintain the number of traveling waves. In this view, they are expected to be initiated whenever the number of waves becomes too low . For this, a hormone-like substance is proposed that circulates rapidly within the animal (green in the simulation below). All pigment-producing cells contribute to the hormone production. In this way a global signal is available throughout the animal that provides information about how many waves exist within the mantle gland and the growing edge.




To initiate a backwards-running wave, cells have to remain in a steady state until the refractory period of the neighboring cells is over.  Whether a pulse-like or steady state activation occurs depends on the ratio of time constants. A shortening of the half-life of the inhibitor can lead to the required transition from the pulse-like into the steady state activation. Therefore, the hormone is assumed to stabilizes the inhibitor. Each activated cell produces the hormone. Whenever the number of traveling waves and thus the hormone concentration become too low (light green phase in the simulation above),  the inhibitor will decay so rapidly that the cells switch from the pulse-like mode into a continuos mode of activation. The neighboring cells can be re-infected after their refractory period is over. This leads to branch initiation and a  doubling of the number of traveling waves. Thus, the hormone concentration rises rapidly and the cells return to normal pulse-like activation that propagates in a wave-like manner (dark-green period).
