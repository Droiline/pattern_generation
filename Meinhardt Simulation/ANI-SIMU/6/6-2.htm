No two Oliva shells have the same pattern
 
 

One will never find two shells of Oliva porphyria with exactly the same pattern. According to the model, minor differences are decisive whether the branch formation actually occurs or not. If a branch was formed the probabilities for further branching changes – a characteristic feature of chaotic processes. Therefore, unavoidable minor differences are sufficient to generate a diversity of patterns. In the following two figures, two simulations, black and red, are superimposed that are initiated with the same initial conditions. Only a different set of random numbers is used. Initially, both patterns are identical with the red pattern covering the black pattern almost completely. With each decision to form or not to form a branch, the patterns become more different and the differences spread to neighbouring regions. In the left simulations, +1% fluctuations have been assumed in the autocatalysis, in the right simulation +10%. As expected, in the latter case the pattern diversifies more rapidly.
