Orientation of cell movement by minute external asymmetries:
 Chemotaxis and the orientation of growth cones


On the previous page is has been described that long filaments of differentiated cells can be generated by a moving signal that leaves behind filaments of differentiated cells. However, in many cases, the filaments are formed by long extensions of single cells. The three pictures below are from a time laps movie showing the extension of a nerve cell in tissue culture. The growth cone at the tip is known to sense directional signals for the orientation of further growth. Another example are the pollen cells in plants that extend long tubes to achieve fertilization.



(by courtesy of Jürgen Löschinger, Tübingen)

Similarly, many cells are able to move as a whole toward a target region. Leukocytes, for instance, have to move to an inflammation site. Cells of Dictyostelium discoideum move toward each other in the case of foot shortage. In all these cases signaling substances are involved. Concentration differences of a few percent across a cell or a growth cone are sufficient for the chemotactic orientation. How it is possible for such minute concentration differences to be detected by the cells?
 

Time-lapse movies such as those shown above demonstrate that this orientation is based on highly dynamic processes. Protrusions are stretched out and retracted in a seemingly irregular way. Many cell types show such dynamic behavior even in the absence of external signals, arguing for mechanisms in which a pattern formation mechanism exists within the cells. The orientation of the internal pattern is oriented by the external signal. This internal pattern causes a rearrangement of the cytoskeleton and changes in the cell shape. Only the generation of this internal pattern in the response to external gradients is described by the model.
 

An activator-inhibitor system is able to detect minute differences: In the first simulation, it is shown that an activator-inhibitor interaction is very sensitive against imposed asymmetries. Assumed is that an external signal (blue) has an asymmetry of 2% over the cell diameter. It has an activating influence on the activator autocatalysis. Despite the fact that 1% random fluctuation in each space element of the cell surface blurs this minute signal, the system is sensitive enough to detect this asymmetry. A local high activator maximum (green) emerges at the side pointing to the highest concentration (black arrow). The self-enhancement is antagonized by the rapidly spreading inhibitor (red):

The second sequence in this simulation shows that the orientation also works with a much smaller absolute level of the external signal (smaller blue circle). This is essential for an orientation in a field that uses graded concentration profiles as guiding cues.  However, as shown in the third sequence, after an establishment of a strong internal signal, even a much stronger and more asymmetric external signal (eccentric blue circle above) is unable to reorient the once established cell-internal pattern (note that the cell copes with the higher external signal by increasing inhibitor level shown in red).

An established internal signal has to be quenched to maintain the sensitivity: Thus the problem is not the detection of the minute signals but the maintenance of the sensitivity towards varying directions of the orienting signal. For this, the internal signals have to disappear in order to give new maxima the chance to arise at a more appropriate position. One possibility is the use of an oscillating patterning system. Such system cycles through a phase in which it is sensitive to an external asymmetry, followed by a phase in which the imposed internal asymmetry is amplified, culminating in a strongly localized signal. The subsequent accumulation of the inhibitor leads to a collapse of the activation. After decay of the inhibitor, the system enters again into the sensitive phase in which a new direction can be chosen. Oscillations do occur if the inhibitor has a longer half-life than the activator:

Several problems remain in such a system. Frequently there is more than one protrusion formed at the time and they are not formed in synchrony. At a given time some protrusions stretch out while others retract. Moreover, the model above would predict short phases in which the cell is sensitive to the external gradient followed by relatively long phases in which the cell is insensitive. This is in contrast to the observation that, e.g., Dictyostelium cells maintain continuously their sensitivity. Moreover, the simple oscillating system of pattern formation works only in a small range of parameters since lateral inhibition requires an overall activation in order that the competition can work. The solution is a separation of the antagonistic reaction that accomplishes lateral inhibition and the antagonistic reaction that causes the disappearance of the once established maxima.
 
The role of saturation in the formation of several hot spots: Autocatalysis saturating at high levels of activation leads to one or more activated regions that increase in size (not in the level of activation) until sufficient inhibitor is produced.

Without fluctuations, the cell-internal signal appears at a coherent region (left); with fluctuations (right), several maxima can appear. Those regions that have the best condition due to the combined action of the external signal and the fluctuations will win. Generally, the patterns of protrusions depend on the strength of the signal, on the activator diffusion and on the degree of the saturation of the activator autocatalysis.

A permanent sensitivity results from a finite half-life of the 'hot spots' [1]: In order to account for the flexibility of the system in response to changing directional signals, the saturating system has to be supplemented by a quenching mechanism. This is introduced by a second antagonistic reaction that acts locally and that has a long time constant. It causes a destabilization of once formed maxima. Since the total activated area is regulated, any local maximum that disappears makes place for a new one. It can emerge at a position governed by the external signal. The result is a permanent creation of hot spots at the side exposed to the highest signal concentration and their disappearance after a certain time interval. In the simulation below, a concentration difference of 2% across the cell and 1% random fluctuation is assumed. Despite the blurring effect of the fluctuation, it is clear that the overall direction is correctly detected, even after a change of the gradient.

Thus, a system consisting of one autocatalytic reaction that is balanced by two antagonistic reactions with different time constants and different spreading allows the generation of local activated regions on the cell cortex, which are highly sensitive towards external signals. The system is able to detect minute external asymmetries and can permanently adapt to changing directions and absolute levels. In the absence of external asymmetries, nevertheless local signals are generated. This leads even the absence of any graded cues to a dynamic regulation of protrusions.
