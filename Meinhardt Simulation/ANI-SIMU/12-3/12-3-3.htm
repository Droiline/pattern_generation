Coordinated cell shape changes in the absence of external signals:
the past becomes decisive

It is known since long that chemotactic cells and growth cones maintain their dynamic cell changes even in the absence of external signals. This was an essential reason to assume that not only an amplification of an external asymmetry takes place but also that the cells have an internal patterning system. This enables a cell to detect minute concentration differences but also cause that in the absence of a signal, e.g., random fluctuation are sufficient to initiate the cell-internal patterning.

A very remarkable observation of group of Michael Vicker was that in the slime mould Dictyostelium discoideum these shape changes are by no means random.  In terms of the model, in the absence of an external signal the cells are so sensitive that the remnants of the poisoning antagonist are sufficient for orientation. Therefore, a once formed protrusion influences the subsequent one and are, therefore, not random. Two modes are especially frequent. In the first, protrusions stretch out at two opposing sides. After their retraction a new pair results with a 90° orientation with respect to the preceding one. The following figures show the outline of an isolated cell in 3s interval:

The second mode leads to protrusions that move around the cells in a wave-like manner. The cell seems to rotate.

The first mode corresponds to the out-of-phase oscillation, the second to the traveling wave mode, both discussed above for particular shell patterns. The following simulation shows the pattern formation on a ring using the same equation and essentially the same constants. Assumed is an oscillating activator-depleted substrate system (green-gray). The diffusible inhibitor (red) has likewise also a long time constant. The size of the 'cell' is such that two peaks are possible. The long-lasting poisoning leads to break down of the peaks at opposite positions and the next trigger appears at 90°. Even after starting from a homogeneous situation, this mode is rapidly attained.

With a slightly higher rate of substrate production, the maxima become more stable. They do not break down but become shifted into a neighboring position. The locally rising inhibitor also causes there a poisoning, enforcing a further shift, and so on. The result is a rotating pattern, in full agreement with the observation:
