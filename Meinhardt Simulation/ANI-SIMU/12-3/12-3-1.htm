Formation of net-like structures




Structures consisting of long filaments that branch are very frequent in development. Nerve nets,  blood vessels, tracheae in insects and  the veins of leaves are examples (below from left to right  [1]). The elements can consist of single very long extended cells (as in nerves) or of long chains of differentiated cells (as in leaves).

According to the model proposed, filaments appear like traces behind local signals that wander over the field.

In the simulation above, it is assumed that local signals are generated by an activator - inhibitor system (red spots). When these signals are above a certain threshold, the exposed cells differentiate into members of the filament system (blue squares). Differentiated cells repel the signal, causing in this way their shift into a neighboring region. These cells also differentiate and the filament becomes elongated, and so on. To orient the elongation into a region not supplied by filaments, all cells are supposed to produce a substrate (green in the simulation above, brown in the simulation below) that is removed by the differentiated cells. The substrate concentration is a measure of how urgently the cells need additional veins or filaments, for instance, in order to remove an oxygen deficiency. Due to the activator-inhibitor mechanism, there is a competition along the filament. Those regions will win that contain the highest substrate concentration, causing a corresponding elongation. This is usually the tip region of a filament. In the following simulation the distributions of the four substances involved are plotted.

green/red: activator-inhibitor system that produces the signal for the new differentiation
brown: substrate removed by the differentiated cells. Activator signal is shifted towards higher substrate concentrations
blue: state of cell differentiation, high = member of the vessel system

The simulation above also shows how branches are formed. A basic activator production in the differentiated cells can lead to a trigger of a new maximum along an existing filament whenever the level of the local inhibitor concentration becomes low enough. This is the case if other elongation points are sufficiently remote and if sufficient substrate is available.

Many net-like structures are able to regulate. Filaments in the non-damaged neighborhood start branching and filaments grow in into the damaged region to repair the defect. Pattern regulation after a damage in a tracheal network is  an example:

Repair of a damaged net (drawn after [2,1]). (a) The tracheal system in the ventral abdomen of a normal, undamaged bug. (b) Oxygen supply to the fourth segment is disrupted by cutting the corresponding trachea (broken line, site marked by the arrow in Fig. a). Oxygen-deprived epithelial cells attract new filaments to repair the damage. In this way, the oxygen supply but not the natural pattern is restored


In the model, after removal of some filaments, the substrate is no longer removed and the rising substrate concentration attracts activator maxima from the non-injured region. The regenerated pattern is similar but not identical to the original one, demonstrating that not the final pattern but the mode of generation is controlled.



Several factors are known that play a role in the determination of final density of the net. For instance, a tumor can only grow if it is able to attract new blood vessels for its nourishment. In contrast, in cartilage vessels disappear, contributing to the white appearance of that tissue. In an experiment by Folkman [3], a piece of tumor tissue was implanted into a rabbit retina. It attracted new vessels (below right). If, however, between the tumor (T) and the existing vessels a piece of cartilage (C) is implanted, the cartilage repels newly formed vessels (left).

In leaf venation, frequently branches from the midrib are formed on alternating sides. The example below shows that this rule is mostly but not strictly obeyed:

According to the model, whether a branch is formed towards one side or the other depends on minute fluctuations. However, if a branch has been formed, let us say, to the right, the next branch will probably point to the left due to the substrate removal by the first branch. On some leaves there are cases where branches to both sides have their origin at the same position of the midrip. According to the model, this can occur if the initiating maximum splits and give rise to two traveling signals, one to each side. The alternation in the branching direction can be seen in the two simulations below. If the details of a pattern depend on fluctuations but each step has a strong influence on forthcoming decisions, the actual pattern will be unpredictable. Indeed, the venation of two leaves on the same tree is never identical although they are certainly formed under control of the same genetic information. It is a feature of this version of the model that the filaments of networks end mostly blind. This is in agreement with the lymph vessels and tracheae. Other systems, however, form frequently anastomoses. The veins of the leaves in higher plants form such reconnections:
 
 

The exact mechanism is still an open problem. Reconnections were presumably a major achievement during plant evolution since evolutionary earlier plants such as the Ginkgo didn't have them.

The many veins on the wings of dragon flies (below, right) consist of closed loops only. Also the early plexus of blood vessels in vertebrates consist of such loops. A possible mechanism to create such a pattern is a combination of a spot and a stripe system: The spots act as a system of inhibitory islands dictating where no filaments are to be formed [4].  Closed nets of filaments arise at the maximum distance from the inhibiting spots. The regions that are free of vessels are, therefore, assumed to play an active role. This is in agreement with the shaping of the vessels by growing holes in the plexus during blood vessel formation [5]. In the following simulation, the spot-system is shown in blue. It has an inhibiting influence on a stripe-forming system (red). To illustrate the closed loops more clearly, in the last part of the sequence the spots are no longer drawn.
