Gene activation under the influence
of morphogenetic signaling



The generation of signals by the movement of molecules via diffusion works only in small fields. In larger fields the time required to generate patterns by randomly moving molecules would be much too long. Therefore, the signals generated at small scales have to be translated into more permanent states of cell differentiation that can be maintained upon further growth. The obvious means is a stable concentration- (and thus space-) dependent activation of genes. Mechanisms to generate graded concentration profiles have been discussed above. On this and the subsequent page, the concentration-dependent activation of genes will be illustrated.

The simplest situation is the activation of a particular gene whenever the local concentration of a signaling molecule (generally termed a morphogen) exceeds a threshold. This is possible if a gene product has a positive feedback on the activation of its own gene. In the following simulation, the signal is shown in green, the level of gene activation is given in red. The gene activation occurs most rapidly in the region of highest signal concentration.  Below a certain threshold the cells don't switch even after an indefinitely long time. Thus, although the gradient is smooth, the resulting gene activation has a sharp border. This border does not change after the morphogenetic signal is reduced or disappears. How such a sharp threshold can arise can be explained on the basis of the equation and the illustration given below:
 
The gene activator a has a positive feedback on its own synthesis that saturates at high levels. It is cell- local, i.e., no diffusion takes place as it is appropriate for a transcription factor; m is the concentration of the external signal, i.e., the local concentration of the morphogen gradient. With ra = 0.05, sa = 0.02; 
the switch occurs at about  m = 0.01
At low levels of the gene activator (below the yellow spot in the figure below) and in the absence of any external signal m, the linear decay term is dominating. The concentration will decrease further (arrow to the left). In contrast, at higher levels (above the yellow dot) the positive feedback is dominating and the level will increase until the saturation comes into play. Thus, there are only two stable states, low and high (pink dots). With an external morphogen, the curve shifts (red). The low stable steady state may disappear. The concentration of the gene activator rises until the upper state is reached. The latter will be maintained even if the morphogen is no longer available: the cell changed its differentiation permanently.
