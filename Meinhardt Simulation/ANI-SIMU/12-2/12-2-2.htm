The activation of several genes under the influence of a morphogen gradient

Frequently several genes are activated by a singe morphogen in a space-dependent manner. To find molecularly feasible mechanisms, the insight was essential that that gene activation and pattern formation in space share formal features. In spatial pattern formation, typically cells in a small region become activated, while the remaining cells at other positions are inhibited. Analogously for gene activation, imagine that in a particular situation the genes A, B, C or D can be activated. To elicit a particular cell differentiation gene C, for instance, has to become activated and the alternative genes A, B and D should be repressed (their activation is required at different positions). Thus, the selection of a particular gene can be regarded as a pattern formation among alternative genes. Based on this formal analogy, gene activation was expected to be based on autoregulation - as the self-enhancement is usually called in gene activation - and mutual competition. Space-dependent gene activation results from a coupling between a pattern formation in space and a pattern formation among alternative genes. By molecular-genetic observations meanwhile many autoregulatory genes have been found.

Cells become ‘promoted':
For position-dependent gene activation under the influence of a graded signal, a particular concentration range has to lead to the activation of a particular gene. An analysis of experiments with early insect development has suggested that measuring the concentration is not a single and instantaneous event, but instead proceeds step by step until a state is achieved that corresponds to the local morphogen concentration. An analogy will provide some intuition as to how unidirectional promotion works:

Let us assume a barrel at the base of a staircase. A flood can lift it up to a higher level, where it will remain even when the flood recedes. The position of the barrel is a measure for the highest level the flood has attained. A higher flood at later times can lift the barrel to an even higher level. In contrast, a second lower flood has no influence. The barrel remains where it came to rest before. Although the flood can vary continuously, the barrel comes to rest only at very particular level. After a near miss of one level, only the next lower level is possible.

Starting with a default activation of, for instance, the gene A, gene B becomes activated in the region in which the morphogen concentration surpasses a particular threshold level. In this process, gene A can be switched off due to a mutual competition. In a still smaller region, gene C will become active, and so on. Each of these steps is essentially irreversible. The simulation below shows such stepwise gene activation. Although only small concentration differences exist in neighboring cells, sharp borders are formed in which either one or another gene becomes active.

The switching from one gene to the next occurs more rapidly in regions of higher morphogen concentrations. This leads to a wave-like time course of gene activation as it is observed in many systems. This is, however, not a real wave based on a sequential triggering of neighboring cells but cells exposed to a higher morphogen concentration switch more rapidly. In the simulation below several snapshots are given. Gene activation is indicated by pixel densities. The movement of the activities and the sharpening of the borders over time are easily seen.

The actual implementation of the mechanism has to cope with an apparent paradox. Imagine gene A is the default gene and that the genes B, C.. should become activated at higher and higher morphogen concentrations. Gene C requires, therefore, a higher signal than gene B. Thus, the activation of gene C is less sensitive in respect to the signal than gene B. Why then is gene C activated at all? And why it is able to switch off gene B that responds more sensitively to the signal? During the modeling it has turned out that this problem requires quantitative differences in the ability of the genes for autoregulation: Gene C, although it is less sensitive to the signal than gene B, can take over since it is more efficient in its autoregulation. This will occur whenever the signal is high enough.
 
All genes involved are assumed to follow the same rules. Only the equations for the gene product b and c are given as examples. Each gene product a, b, c,.. has an autocatalytic feedback on its own gene and an inhibitory action on the activation of alternative genes. This has the consequence that within a particular cell only one of the alternative genes can remain active. The self-inhibition of a gene has the character of a saturation and leads to an upper limit of the activation that a gene can obtain. The morphogen m has an additional activating influence on the genes. In this example it is assumed that the activation of the gene b requires the presence of the gene product of the gene a. sa, sb, sc... are the coupling constants for the self-amplification. For a position-dependent gene activation it is required that 'higher' genes are less sensitive to the morphogen but better in the mutual competition, i.e., the relation sc>sb>sa must be satisfied ra, rb...= 0.01, sa = 1; sb = 1.42; sc = 2,..

The biological significance of this unidirectional promotion is easy to understand. If a graded distribution is generated by a local source in conjunction with diffusion, any growth enlarges the distance between a given cell and the source. The morphogen concentration will generally decrease in the course of time. And yet, if activated genes remain activated upon a lowering of the morphogen concentration, an existing subdivision will be maintained even in the face of growth or stretching of the tissue domain: After the interpretation is complete, the signal is no longer necessary and can be turned off. Several biological systems are known that show this type of regulation (references are in the book). The simulation below shows three snapshots of a gene system as described above in a uniformly growing tissue. The space-dependent activation of particular genes is controlled by the inhibitor (red) of an activator-inhibitor system. In the center (marked by arrows) the signal becomes lower and lower. Nevertheless, the regions of different gene activities are maintained (the trigger of a new activator maximum is suppressed by a small baseline inhibitor production).

Also clearly visible in this simulation is that as long as the signal is about to promote the transition, a cross-activation with the next ‘higher’ gene exists (upper figure). This cross-activation disappears when the signal becomes anyway too low to accomplish the next transition.

Two types of regulatory behavior reported in the literature are described by the model in a straightforward manner. First, an increase in the morphogen concentration after the final determination is already obtained leads to a distal or posterior transformation. In contrast, as mentioned above, a lowering remains without effect. Experimentally realized are such changes in the morphogen concentration by transplantations either towards or away from the source or by implantation of morphogen-releasing tissue. A biological example for that behavior can be found in Nieuwkoop's experiment with early amphibian embryos. In Xenopus he removed organizing regions very early and found less posterior structures. He concluded, that posterior transformation depends on the duration that the organizing tissue is in contact with the tissue to be specified. In terms of the model, time plays a role, but the promotion comes to rest at a concentration-specific level that is not exceed even when the cells are exposed to the transforming signal for an extended period.
