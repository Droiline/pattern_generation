If waves prefer to die instead of colliding
 



The shells shown above display oblique branching lines that terminate without any collision with counter-waves. This termination is not announced by a retardation in the spread of pigmentation, i.e., by a bending of the lines towards a more perpendicular orientation in relation to the growing edge. This suggests that the activation is anyway near the lowest level at which the chain of triggering events can be maintained. Any additional lowering causes termination even in cells that have not been activated for a long time. Termination occurs preferentially if two waves approach each other. Either one or both waves terminate. In the latter case, a non-activated gap remains. This suggests that a rapidly spreading inhibition emanates from the activated cells. A counter wave that senses this inhibition may terminate.

A spontaneous trigger of a new activation occurs preferentially in the gap left untouched by two terminated waves (red arrows on shells shown above). This behavior appears counterintuitive. On one hand, the inhibition is so strong that wave termination takes place even without collision. On the other hand, a spontaneous activation is possible somewhat later in the same cells that could not be triggered earlier by activated neighboring cells.
 
 

This suggests that a third, very long-lasting, non-diffusible inhibition exists that provides a measure for the time passed since the last activation (blue in the simulation above). A spontaneous activation becomes possible again only when most of this inhibitor disappeared. The second diffusible inhibitor (pink in the simulations) is almost completely gone at this stage. The long lasting inhibitor must be non-diffusible otherwise the information on the location of the gap would be lost. Therefore, these two inhibitions have different properties and connot be combined to a single agend. The long-lasting but local inhibitor also has another function. Similarly as discussed for penetrating waves, the local accumulation of this inhibitor also forces pigment activation to move into a neighboring region even though this region is already poisoned by the diffusible inhibitor (on its own, a rapidly diffusing inhibitor would tend to generate spatially stable patterns). The pushing effect of the non-diffusible inhibitor and the poisoning effect of the diffusible inhibitor together may be responsible for the situation in which activation is at the borderline of its maintenance.

The simulations below show the termination of two approaching waves and the triggering of a new wave in the remaining gap. It appears at the lowest concentration of the long-lasting inhibitor (blue). The termination results from the long-ranging inhibitor (pink). Left: a space-time plot, right: situation at the growing edge. The red bar is the activator of the modifying oscillation (which is responsible for fine structure including branch formation)

Considering all these features, at least three inhibitions must be involved to accomplish fine branching lines that terminate but re-trigger spontaneously in the gaps:
(i) A very rapid and non-diffusible inhibition that causes activation to last for a short time interval only (b in equation, see box; not shown in the graphics). It determines the thickness of the oblique lines. Its diffusion (if any) must be much smaller than that of the activator; otherwise no traveling waves would form.
(ii) A rapidly diffusing inhibitor with a longer time constant whose overall accumulation poisons a region. This causes wave termination when two waves approach (e in the equation).
(iii) A very long lasting, non-diffusible inhibition responsible for the shift of activation despite the diffusible inhibitor (f  in the equation). It allows a re-activation only after a long non-activated time interval. This occurs, therefore,  preferentially in the gaps left open by earlier waves.
