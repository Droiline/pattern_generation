Sierpinsky-Triangles on Cymbiola innexa
 

The short signal that should lead to transition into pulse-like activation (fine red lines  in the upper part of the simulation at right) may be frequently insufficient and the activation (brown) reappears immediately. If successful, however, the sudden pigment termination would lead to a white triangle. This is relatively frequent in small areas, giving rise to the small triangles. It may also occur, although more rarely, over a larger area leading to large triangles. The resulting patterns are similar to the well known Sierpinsky triangle with its fractal geometry. These structures are self-similar. The triangle below results from an iterative process in which three  more elementary triangles are combined to a new triangle that is subsequently is rescaled (detailed information for Sierpinsky triangles are available in the world-wide web).

rc= 0.1; bc = 0.08; sc = 0.01; rd = 0; bd =.05 
The white triangles result from a block of substrate production during the oscillation; sb = 2.4. The resulting patterns are parameter-sensitive. The figures at bottom are calculated with sb = 2.5 and 2.3 respectively. 

Again, these simulations are very parameter-sensitive. A stronger block of substrate production leads to a dramatically higher rate for the transition from a high to a low steady state. This suggests that other regulatory mechanisms exist that maintain the average pigmentation density by a more global control.
