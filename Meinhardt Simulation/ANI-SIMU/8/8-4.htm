The ambiguity between triangles and branching lines



The two shells below left are of the same species. One displays triangles, the other branched lines. On the shell at right, both patterns are visible on the same shell, with branches formed at the younger and triangles at older age. This coexistence is very remarkable since both pattern elements require contradictory parameter changes by a modifying reaction. As explained above, branch formation requires the temporary transition into a steady state. If the pigmentation reaction remains active for a sufficiently long time interval, a backwards-running waves can be triggered (see, for instance, Oliva porphyria). This occurs if temporary either the activator obtains a longer half-life or the antagonistic reaction a shorter half-life. In contrast, as explained in this chapter, triangle formation requires that normally the cells remain in a spreading steady state. A short pulse of a modifying reaction shifts the pigmentation system from the steady state mode into a pulse-like mode, causing the large-scale breakdown. Thus, while branch formation needs a temporary transition from the traveling wave into the steady state mode, triangle formation requires exactly the opposite.
 


These conflicting requirements can be resolved under the assumption that both modifications, extinguishing and enhancement, occur in rapid succession. So far, we have assumed that the activator of the modifying system (red horizontal lines) shortens the half-life of the pigment activator, causing a transition from a steady state to a pulse-like activation. The branching phenomenon suggests that the antagonist of the modifying reaction, for instance an inhibitor (green horizontal lines in the figures below), plays the opposite role in this modification. While the short pulse of the activator acts as an extinguishing signal to form the lower border of the triangle, the longer lasting inhibitor that immediately follows shifts the pigment system into the  steady state mode.

In the figure above, it is assumed that the unperturbed pigmentation reaction remains in a steady state. The activator of the oscillating reaction shortens the half-life of the pigment reaction, causing termination and the lower edge of the triangle. The rapidly following high inhibitor concentration has the opposite effect, elongating the half-life of the pigment activator. This brings the system rapidly back to the steady state. In this simulation, the frequent termination at the inner side of the triangles result from the accumulation of an inhibitor (e in the equation below). In cells that were more recently activated have a reduced chance to survive the next breakdown. Without this inhibition, the whole shell would become covered with connected triangles:

So much for the triangles. What about branches? Oblique lines are formed if the ratio of the time constants in the pigment reaction is such that only a pulse-like activation is possible that spreads in a wave-like manner. The short reduction in the activator half-life will go almost unnoticed  while its subsequent elongation by the inhibitor can lead to the initiation of branches:

Comparing the branch-simulations above with natural patterns reveals a characteristic difference: many oblique lines end blindly, especially if two lines are close to each other. This requires a modification that is described on the next page.
