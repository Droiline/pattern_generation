Triangles also need a long-ranging antagonist



The most surprising feature of connected triangles is the almost immediate spread of pigmentation after a collective breakdown - the bottom side of the triangle. Usually there is no lag between the breakdown and the spread of pigmentation into the same region again. Thus, the signal for a transition from the steady state to the pulse-wise mode and back to the steady state of pigment formation must be very short. The system returns to its normal condition of bistable activation very rapidly. This tells us that the antagonistic reaction must have a very short time constant  such that the new steady state is found rapidly even after such a severe event as the large-scale breakdown. A similar feature was mentioned for Oliva porphyria in connection with the branching of lines. In that case, backward waves proceeded immediately after their birth with the same speed as the original wave, although they were in the shadow of the primary wave.
 


A consequence of such a short time constant of the antagonist is that a spontaneous trigger of a new chain of triangles is not explicable by the minimum model (and did not occur in the simulations given on the preceding pages). This phenomenon – an example is given on the shell above – indicates that a second, longer lasting antagonist is at work. If it is, for instance, an inhibitor, its disappearance is the precondition to trigger the pigmentation again after a longer pause, initiating one or two diverging chains of triangles.

As shown in the simulation above, the employment of a second,  long-ranging antagonist (pink in the simulation above) solves another problem. The pattern can arise spontaneously. It does not require local initiation sites since the long-ranging inhibitor can accomplish the initiating pattern formation. Moreover, in a chain of triangles, one side of the triangle was exposed more recently to an activation. Thus, this side experiences a higher inhibition. Therefore, the other side of the triangle has a better chance to survive the breakdown. This contributes to the formation of long chains of oblique triangles.

With this long-ranging inhibition, the condition for crossings and branching are satisfied as discussed in chapter 5. Actually a closer inspection of the patterns discussed in chapter 5 shows the involvement of  an oscillating pattern that is visible in the relief-like modulation of the shell surface. The merging of two such horizontal lines on the shell shown in the center indicates that this oscillation is nearly but not completely synchronous. The lower edges of the triangular fine structure coincide with the fine lines of the horizontal surface structure.
