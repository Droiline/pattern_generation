Generation and regeneration of graded distributions

If the range of the activator is of the order of the field size, only a single marginal maximum can emerge. This is assumed to be an  important feature for the understanding of development in higher organisms. Even if initially no pattern is present, cells on one side of a field obtain a different signal than those at the other. An activator maximum has essential properties of an embryonic organizer.






According to the model, a certain field size has to be surpassed until pattern formation can occur. In a field smaller than the range of the activator, the activator and the inhibitor are in a stable dynamic equilibrium with each other. The simulation below shows the onset of polar pattern formation after a certain extension of the field is surpassed:


Many early developmental systems regulate. An example is the regeneration of  missing structures. This is an inherent feature of our model for pattern formation. After removal of the activated region - the organizer -  inhibitor is no longer produced in the remaining fragment. The remnant inhibitor will decay until a new activation is triggered from a baseline activator production. The subsequently produced inhibitor shapes the regenerating gradient. In the simulation below, a field with a pattern is cut into halves. Eventually in both fragments the same pattern is formed:

Graded distributions are presumably also involved in the formation of the snail shells. The shells are highly asymmetric; one side makes the shoulder. Further, on many shells two colored bands appear at reproducible positions. The three shells shown below provide an illustration for asymmetry and banding. Early in the development of the snail a graded distribution of a signaling substance may provide the signals to specify the corresponding structures. This may involve position-specific activation of genes.
