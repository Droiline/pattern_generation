Pattern formation in two-dimensional field

 

Graded distributions emerge also in sheets of cells if the range of the activator is comparable with the field size. In a near circular field and  in the absences of external asymmetries, a high concentration appears at an arbitrary marginal position. Again, the field obtains a polarity. A graded distribution emerges along one axis while perpendicular to the steepest slope the concentrations are roughly constant:



In a sheet with an elongated geometry, it is a property of the system that the gradient forms along the largest extension of the field. The highest and lowest activator concentration appear at the most remote positions:

This is presumably important for pattern formation in single cells. In many dividing cells, the division plane is formed perpendicular to their longest extension. Our pattern-forming mechanism allows detecting the largest extension of an elongated structure.

If the field size is larger than the range of the inhibitor, multiple maxima emerge. If initiated by random fluctuations, the spacing is not very regular but a maximum and minimum spacing is maintained.



While usually pattern formation on shells is a one-dimensional process, on some shells two-dimensional patterning plays a role. These shells are covered with the periostracum – a protrusion of the ectoderm of the mollusk. These two protrusions meet somewhere on the shell opposite to the opening, leading to the seam-like line that is clearly visible on the shells shown below. A pattern generated in this cell sheet is copied to the underlying shell. This is therefore a two-dimensional patterning process, leading to very different types of patterns.
 
 





On some shells, pattern formation proceeds at early stages of the mollusk in the space-time mode mentioned above. At adult stages, however, a transition occurs into the two-dimensional mode. Although formed on a single shell, both patterns have a very different character. The shell of Cypraea diluculum is an example The left shell has a typical space-time pattern, in contrast to the dot-like pattern around the shell opening visible on the right shell:

If the autocatalysis saturates at some maximal level of local activation, preferentially stripes are formed. Since initiated by random fluctuations, these stripes have no preferential orientation; they meander and bifurcate. With saturation the activated regions enlarge. Since lateral inhibition is still at work, activated regions require non-activated regions in the neighborhood into which the inhibitor can diffuse. These to features seem to exclude each other. Stripes, however, satisfies both requirements since activated cells have activated neighbors and non-activated cells are nearby into which the inhibitor can escape:




Midline formation of higher, bilateral-symmetric organism require the formation of a single straight stripe.  This can be achieved by a cooperation of stripe-forming and a spot forming mechanism.

The formation of the stripes requires some diffusion of the activator, enforcing that at least some neighbors of an activated cell becomes also activated. Without this diffusion-based community effect but still with saturation, a salt-and-pepper distribution will result. Activation will become an all-or-nothing event. The number of activated cells is a certain portion of the total number of cells. Such a mechanism allows for the generation of differentiated cells in a regulated proportion but in an intermingled arrangement. The prestalk cells in the slime mold Dictyostelium discoidem are an example. Due to the saturation, there is no minimum spacing between the activated cells although lateral inhibition is involved:

The following example shows a simple model for phyllotaxis. Assumed is a cylindrical array of cells that grows at its upper end. First a maximum is formed in the ring-shaped growth zone, providing a signal for leaf initiation. Whenever newly formed cells in the growth zone obtained sufficient distance from the existing leaf primordia, a new signal is triggered. In this case, leaves appear in a 180 degree position. Helical arrangements of leaves suggest, however, that the mechanism is more complex (see phyllotaxis).
