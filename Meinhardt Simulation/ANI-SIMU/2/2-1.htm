Patterns that are stable in time: local self-enhancement and long-range inhibition as the driving force





For pattern formation in biology,  the generation of local concentration maxima of molecules is a crucial step. These maxima act as signals to initiate particular structures at corresponding  positions. Pattern formation requires local self-enhancement and long-range inhibition. The self-enhancement has the consequence that small deviations from a homogeneous distribution grow further. The long-ranging antagonistic reaction cause that this local increase is connected with a decrease in the surrounding, leading to an overall stability of the emerging pattern. As it will be discussed in this chapter, this allows the generation graded distributions and periodic patterns. Graded distributions play a pivotal role in the early embryonic development since they allow the generation of positional information in an initially equivalent array of cells. By local autocatalysis and long-range inhibition primary organizing regions can be generated and in this way  embryonic axes can be established. In contrast, in shell patterning periodic structures are frequently involved.

A typical pattern forming reaction consists of an autocatalytic activator (green) and its long-ranging antagonist, the inhibitor (red) :



As shown in the simulation above, periodic patterns emerge if the range of the inhibitor is smaller than the extension of the field in which the pattern formation takes place. The range is given by the average distance a molecule can travel between its synthesis and removal. It depends on its rate of spreading, for instance, by diffusion and on its half-life. A longer living molecule can travel over a longer distance. The blue squares is the simulation above denote the source density or competence, i.e., the ability of the cell to perform the pattern forming reaction. Small random fluctuations in this ability are sufficient to initiate pattern formation. If initially two peaks arise too close to each other, one becomes suppressed by its dominating neighbor. The pattern is, therefore, somewhat irregular but a maximum and minimum distance is maintained. Although the final pattern is stable in time, it depends on a permanent production and destruction of molecules.

The actual biochemical implementation of a pattern forming reaction can be more complex involving, ligands, receptors, transcription factors and cascades that transmit the signal from the cell membrane to the nucleus. The stipulation is, however, that these chains can be grouped into a short-ranging positive loop and a long-ranging negative loop. The term ‘Activator’ and ‘Inhibitor’ may stay for such chains of interaction.

Two conditions are essential for the generation of stable patterns:

 
(i) the range of the inhibitor must be much larger than that of the self-enhancing component, the activator.
 
(ii) The inhibitor has to react rapidly to a change of the activator concentration, otherwise oscillations will occur. In terms of the reaction scheme given above, the half-life of the inhibitor must be shorter than that of the activator.
 
On the shell shown below the main part of the pattern was generated by a stable periodic pattern in space leading to stripes perpendicular to the growing edge. The pattern on the shoulder, however, resulted from a periodic pattern in time: stripes parallel to the growing edge. This suggests that minor changes can lead to a transition from one mode into the other.
