Staggered dots along oblique lines:
the regular displacement of activated regions

Another frequent pattern with a periodicity in space and time consists of rows of staggered dots:

Activation in discrete patches suggests that both antagonists diffuse. This can lead to patch-like activations that are separated along the space-  and along time-coordinate. This pattern occurs in a wide range of parameters if both antagonists are inhibitors (Equation 5.3 of the book).



It is clearly visible that after the trigger of an activation (black), one of the inhibitors (red) spreads rapidly. The other (green) spreads only slowly but remains for a longer period. New activations are possible only when the concentrations of both inhibitors become low enough (white regions).

As mentioned, in the activator-inhibitor scheme new maxima are inserted preferentially at some distance from existing activated regions (see insertion of maxima during growth). This is what happens in the staggered dot patterns, except that the influence of existing maxima don' t fade away due to increasing distances but since they disappear after a short time interval due to the accumulation of the long-lasting inhibitor. With a moderate diffusion rate of the long-lasting inhibitor, traveling waves are no longer possible.

In the animated simulation shown below it is clearly visible that the new peaks appear at one side of the former peaks. There the long lasting inhibition (green) is the lowest. After each new activation, the minimum becomes displaced to the left:

With the increase of the diffusion of the long lasting inhibitor, the new maxima appear at larger and larger distances from the preceding maxima. However, the maximum cannot be shifted into a region exposed to the inhibitor of the neighboring peak. Thus, the maxima appear precisely  between the activations of the preceding row (below right).

In this equation, the second antagonist acts on both the activator and the inhibitor production. Thus, the activator concentration remains in a wide range independent of c. This destabilization is in contrast to a stabilization to maintain a single organizing region in a growing field.

This mechanism  - the destabilization of established maxima - will play a major role also the discussion of other basic biological processes, for instance in phyllotaxis and in the orientation of chemotactic cells and growth cones.

In E.coli the selection of a new division site is based on a similar process.  High concentrations of an inhibitor of new division sites jump from one pole to the other and back, leaving only the center as a new division site. For this reaction, many molecular details are known. The corresponding model describe the observations in fine details, see http://www.pnas.org/cgi/content/full/98/25/14202/DC1
