Simulations for chapter 5

Crossings, meshwork of oblique lines and staggered dots:
the combined action of two antagonists



Many shells display simple patterns that require the combined action of two antagonists. Oblique lines that cross each other, net-like patterns, chains of dots arranged along oblique lines and chessboard-like  patterns belong to this group.

    

These patterns are explicable under the assumption that two antagonists are at work: one that is responsible for the pattern in space, the other for the pattern in time. Therefore, one antagonist must have a long range and a short time constant as required for the generation of stable spatial patterns. In contrast, the other must have a long time constant but a short range to generate a pattern in time. Due to their combined action, the local activation maxima become unstable. Either they disappear and reappear at displaced positions (oblique row of dots) or they are permanently shifted into a neighboring position (oblique lines). Each of these antagonistic reactions can be based on an inhibitor, produced at the activated region, or from a substrate, produced everywhere and that is consumed during activation.
