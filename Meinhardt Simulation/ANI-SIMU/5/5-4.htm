Penetration of waves without annihilation: Crossing of oblique lines

A mentioned earlier, usually two colliding waves annihilate each other,  leading to the V-shaped patterns on the shell. Some shells show, however, crossing lines:

Crossings of oblique lines testify that in these cases waves can penetrate each other without annihilation - a very unusual behavior of waves in excitable media. It is a property of the one activator-two antagonist mechanism to generate this pattern:

In the simulation above, an activator-substrate model is assumed that is tuned to bistability. High activation spreads due to activator diffusion. The high state is reached with some overshoot. The additional diffusible inhibitor (red) causes each subsequently activated cell to down regulate the activation in previously activated cells. Apparently normal traveling waves result. However, at the point of collision the activation survives due to the rapid decrease of the additional inhibitor. After recovery of the substrate (green), two new waves are initiated. In the time record, this leads to the crossings:

 

It is a feature of such a system that the borders reflect the waves. Since the borders are assumed to be impermeable, they act like a mirror, i.e., as if a virtual wave approaches the border from the other side of the border. This satisfies the condition that no loss or gain takes place across the borders. This mirror-like reflection of the waves is clearly visible in the simulation shown above.
 
Activator-inhibitor-substrate system, Equation 5.1 abc
Da = 0.01; ra = 0.08; ba =0.0001 ; sa = .5; 
sb = 1; sc = 3; s = ra + 5% random fluctuation
Db = 0; rb = 0.005; bb = 0.1; Dc = 0.4; rc = 0.02.
Tendency for branch formation increases with saturation of autocatalysis (sa =1), shorter time constant of the inhibitor  (rc=0.1) and a stronger inhibitory influence of the latter. Due to the Michaelis-Menten type constant sb very low levels of c remain without effect.
For the simulation of the perturbation at the end of this page, the following parameters have been used:
Da = 0.007; ra = 0.08; ba =0.001 ; sa = 1; sb = 1; 
sc = 11; Db = 0; rb = 003; bb = 0.08; Dc = 0.4; rc = 0.1.
With these parameters one wave direction tends to dominate. 

The following simulation shows a similar situation in a larger field with different initialization.




The model accounts for regulatory features that can be deduced from the shell of Tapes literatus shown below. The pattern  displays a global irregularity. Many waves vanished at the same time. Other waves survived and even formed new waves by wave splitting. The model resolves this apparently contradictory behavior between the extinction of waves on the one hand and the simultaneous generation of new waves on the other. According to the model,  temporary lowering of the activator can lead either to wave termination or, due to the concomitant reduction of inhibitor concentration, to the initialization of a new pair of waves, as occurring during a  collision. In some cases, the attempt to form two waves is clearly visible but one wave did not survive due to the mutual competition.
 
 




Although, as a rule, both waves survive a collision, in some instances one of the new waves becomes extinct, causing a pattern like an amputated X (figures below left).  Again, this provides strong support for the involvement of lateral inhibition in this type of wave formation. Immediately after wave penetration, the two waves still exert an inhibitory influence onto each other.

Since the system can temporary remain in a steady state (as occuring during wave penetration), the system is also able to generate spontaneous backwards-running waves and thus braching lines. With somewhat different parameters (see box) the tendency to form branches increases. The simulation below right shows amputated X's and branching:
 
 




The shells below also show a global perturbation. The traveling waves terminate but new waves are initiated that spread to both sides. On the right shell, some pigment deposition was maintained during the perturbation. It is clearly visible that this occurred at the front side of the wave.

This is also reproduced in the simulation. The activation is highest at the wave front. Therefore, during a global reduction of the activation, the chance is there the highest that some activation survives. Both simulations below use the same initial situation and differ only in the degree of activator reduction during the perturbation, to 15% at left and 10% at right:





The right simulation is shown below in a movie-like manner. After the perturbation, the inhibitor (red) decays so rapidly that the re-established activation triggers waves that spread to both sides. Some of the resulting waves subsequently annihilate each other (as can be seen in the left shell shown above). After some time, a preferential orientation of waves may be re-established.

In these simulations, no asymmetry over the field is assumed. In the real situation, the side attached to the older shell part (left in the photographs above) act certainly as a pacemaker by having a higher oscillation frequency. This leads to a predictable orientation of the waves. The bending of the oblique lines result from a systematic retardation as the cells enter regions that are less and less excitable.
