Meshwork-like patterns versus oblique lines

The two shells shown below are of the same species (Theodoxus doricus, Fig. 5.3 of the book).  One shows a mesh-like pattern, the other  displays oblique lines. This suggests that a close relationship between these two patterns exists. In terms of the model, both patterns depend on an alternation of activation along the space- and along the time coordinate.


(Figures courtesy R. Willmann, Kiel)

The following simulation shows the generation of a mesh-like structure. The same interaction is assumed as on the preceding page. However, the inhibitor accomplishes the role the long-range antagonist while the local depletion leads to the destabilization of the maxima. Shown are concentrations at the growing edge as well as the corresponding space-time plots. If two waves run into each other, they do not annihilate but give rise to two new waves that spread again and later collide with counter waves. A regular mesh-like pattern results:

A faster decay of the inhibitor (change of rc from 0.022 to 0.033) leads instead to non-crossing waves and thus to oblique lines. The shorter time constant of the inhibitor suppresses backwards-running waves. The waves are aligned and move preferentially only in one direction.

 Calculated with equation 5.1 and the following parameters:

 
Activator-substrate-inhibitor system, Equation 5.1 abc
Da = 0.01; ra = 0.05; ba =0.005 ; sa = 1; 
sb = 1; sc = 2; Db = 0; rb = 0; bb = 0.03; 
Dc = 0.4; rc = 0.022 or 0.033; 
since bb<ra, the activator-substrate system has on its own the tendency to oscillate. s = ra + 2% random fluctuation.
The inhibitor accomplishes the long-range interaction. Note that the inhibitor acts on the activator production and thus also on the substrate consumption. In terms of general theory, it reduces the source density (compare with the formation of unique organizers)
