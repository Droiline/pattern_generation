Oscillations out of phase: chessboard-like patterns

In the preceding considerations the long-lasting and locally acting antagonist has been assumed to destabilize a stable pattern. One can argue also other way around: the long-lasting antagonist on its own would lead to a oscillation and the additional long-ranging antagonist enforces a de-synchronization. If some cells are a bit delayed in an array of cells oscillating in near-synchrony, this phase difference will increase further due to the inhibitory influence that spreads from the advanced neighbors. It delays the somewhat retarded cells even more. The synchronism breaks down and oscillations out of phase are formed. The resulting pattern is close to a chessboard with its periodicity along both axes.

For the simulation above, again the activator-substrate-inhibitor system equation 5.1 has been used. Different is that the production rate of the diffusible substrate (green) is insufficient to maintain a stable pattern. Thus, both antagonists enforce an oscillating pattern.

The broadness of the peaks depends on saturation. Without saturation, the peaks would be more confined in space and time, leading to more isolated maxima, a pattern that also exists (see also oblique rows of dots).

As can bee seen on the first shell shown on this page, the chessboard pattern is prone to change into a pattern of oblique lines. In the model, this occurs if the high activation shifts into a neighboring position without a preceding breakdown. This happens, for instance, after an increase in the substrate production  (bb from 0.003 to 0.004). This leads to traveling waves and thus to oblique lines on the shell.

On some shells either the initiation or the extinction of  waves occurs out of phase. According to the model, this is caused by the lateral inhibition inherent in this mode of wave formation. The resulting patterns seem to display a discontinuity. The simulation below shows that the model reproduces this phenomenon. Such a 'discontinuity' can be stable over an extended period.
