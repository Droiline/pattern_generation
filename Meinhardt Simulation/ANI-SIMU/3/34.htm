Lines oblique to the growing edge: records of traveling waves
 
 



Oblique lines on the shell result if one cell infects the next, leading to traveling waves. After the activation in a particular cell surpassed a certain level, the activation goes through a full cycle and infects the next cell, and so on. The crucial conditions for wave formation are:

(i) the activator shows some diffusion (to enable the infection the next cell)

(ii) the inhibitor or the substrate is non-diffusible (otherwise the infection of adjacent cells is suppressed due to the spreading antagonistic effect)

(iii) The cells are in an excitable regime such that a small addition of activator is sufficient to trigger a full cycle. That means the cells wait for a trigger.

(iv) The antagonistic reaction must have a longer time constant in order that after the full activation was obtained, it returns to the low level activation, waiting for the next trigger.

(v) In the simple two-component system a pace-maker region is required from which the waves can start

In the following simulation an activator substrate scheme is assumed. The system is just at the borderline where a spontaneous trigger is possible; the cells that are still able to trigger spontaneously act in this case as pacemakers:

Characteristic is that if two waves run into each other, they annihilate each other mutually, since waves cannot enter into the region that is refractory due to the counter wave. On the shells this leads to oblique lines with a Shaped arrangement as seen in the photograph above. The tip of the V is the zone of annihilation. In contrast, after a spontaneous trigger, waves spread to both sides. Further details for this wave formation are given further below.

On some shells, several oblique lines terminate at the same level, indicating that some catastrophic event happens to the mollusk. The following figure at left shows an example:
 
 



As shown in the simulation at right, this behavior is correctly described by the model (only the activation is seen on the shell in the sharp relief like structure, not the green substrate). If the external event leads to a drop in the activation below a certain level, the waves are interrupted. The substrate (green) increases until spontaneously a new trigger occurs. The simulation below illustrates the same situation in a movie like manner. First the regular wave movement can be seen. After termination of the waves (simulated by a 90% reduction of the activator concentration in a part of the field, new waves are formed that annihilate with the surviving waves. After some time, the original pacemaker region becomes dominant again.

As mentioned above, traveling waves can also be generated by activator-inhibitor systems. In the simulation below, the activation (blue) is switched off by the slowly reacting inhibitor (red). This system has no pattern forming capability. Waves have to be initiated by a local trigger. When two waves collide, they annihilate each other.
