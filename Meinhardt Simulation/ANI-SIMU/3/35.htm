Traveling waves need a pacemaker region



With the simple traveling wave mechanism it remains undetermined where the waves are initiated. Thus, a pacemaker region is required. Otherwise the activations can emerge in synchrony. In the following simulation an activator-substrate system it is assumed. The decay of the substrate is such the threshold for a spontaneous trigger is not surpassed. Therefore, the system remains in an excitable state. Only in the left-most cell the substrate production is somewhat higher such that a regular trigger occurs in a periodic fashion. The activation spreads in a wave-like manner over the field. In the time record, these traveling waves lead directly to ordered oblique lines.

If the ordinary decay of the substrate is somewhat lower (rb= .006 instead of rb=.007, equation 2.4), occasionally cells are able to trigger spontaneously somewhere in the field. Each such event leads to two waves that move in opposite directions. Two colliding waves annihilate each other, leading to the typical V-like pattern elements. This behavior is only obtained in a narrow range of parameters.

In the simulation above, the system was just at the borderline where spontaneous triggering is possible. As shown in the simulation below, without any additional substrate decay (rb = 0), all cells reach the threshold, some only a bit earlier (depending on random fluctuations). The small activator diffusion leads to a near synchronization of the oscillation, causing the horizontal lines mentioned above. However, since the left-most cell is still assumed to oscillate a bit faster due to a somewhat higher substrate production, it acts further as a peacemaker region. In the course of time this region brings the rest of the field under its control. This is visible in the simulation at right by a transition from horizontal to oblique lines.Since the cells become more excitable since the substrate obtains higher levels, the speed of the waves is higher. Therefore, the oblique lines are less steep.
