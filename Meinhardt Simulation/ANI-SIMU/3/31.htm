Synchronous oscillations: stripes parallel the growing edge

Stripes parallel to the growing edge result from a time record of near synchronous oscillations:



Autocatalysis coupled to an antagonistic reaction cannot only generate patterns in space but also patterns in time. Oscillations do occur if the antagonistic reaction has a longer time constant (lower decay rate) if compared with the self-enhancing reaction. The following simulation shows the situation in a single cell:



The activator is shown in green. Its increase precedes that of the inhibitor. The decay rate of the inhibitor (rb = 0.04 in the equation below, i.e. 4% of the molecule disappear in each iteration) is smaller than that of the activator (ra = 0.08). Thus, the condition for oscillations is satisfied. The reason for the oscillation is as follows: the inhibitor reacts too slowly to a change in the activator concentration. After an avalanche-like increase of the activator, the inhibitor accumulates until activator synthesis and thus the inhibitor production breaks down. It takes some time until the inhibitor disappeared due to its (slow) decay. After this refractory period is over, a new activation is triggered from a baseline activator production (ba in the equation below).

In an extended field, both a diffusion of the activator and a diffusion of the inhibitor will lead to a synchronization of the oscillation.
