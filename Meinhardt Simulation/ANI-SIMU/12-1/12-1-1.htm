A non-trivial problem: the maintenance of single organizing regions in growing embryos

As mentioned, a maximum generated by  local self-enhancement and long-range inhibition has many properties of an organizing region (chapter 2). In small fields, the maximum appears at one border of the field.  Either the graded activator or inhibitor profile can provide positional information. However, if the region in which the pattern formation takes place grows, multiple maxima will emerge. The graded pattern will change into a symmetric and ultimately into a periodic pattern:

Growth is, however, the standard situation for a developing embryo. Multiple maxima would lead to several organizing regions instead of one and thus possibly to the formation of several partially fused and malformed embryos.

A single maximum can be stabilized if cells distant to an established maximum loose the ability to perform the pattern forming reaction. This ability we have called source density (blue in the simulations below). It corresponds to the observable feature of competence. Thus, an activator maximum is assumed to have a positive and long-ranging feedback on the competence, accomplished, for instance, by a secondary morphogen that controls the source density. Cells distant to a maximum loose the competence. The induction of additional organizing regions becomes less likely. In this way, a single organizing region can be maintained, even when after substantial growth the size of the field exceeds the range of the inhibition. This has also the consequence that even very small fragments are able to regenerate:

(Note that in contrast to the color-coding used in the book, in this simulation, blue is used for the competence.

During regeneration, those cells that were originally closer to the organizing region have an advantage to win the competition due to their higher competence. In other words, the tissue obtains an intrinsic polarity. Regeneration of an organizing region will occur at a predictable position. This head start accelerates the formation of a new organizer significantly since a symmetry breaking is no longer required. Even if the tissue looks overtly uniform, it is not. The simulation above shows the maintenance of a single organizing region during growth and the regeneration according the original polarity. Note that during regeneration remains nearly unchanged. Afterwards it takes time to restore the normal source density (blue) near the newly formed organizing region.

This property of the model fits with the observation in Hydra. A fragment always forms the new head at side pointing in the direction of the original head, the so-called apical side. Depending on the precise position of a cut, the same cells of the gastric region can either form a head, a foot or maintain the character of the body column:

 
The following simulation shows that a secondary maximum could form if the competence would not decline steeply enough with increasing distance from the organizer:

According to this model, an organizing region has two conflicting influences on the surrounding tissue. On the one hand it prevents the formation of additional organizing regions in the surrounding tissues by the long-ranging inhibitory effect. On the other hand, it also has an activating influence by keeping surrounding cells competent. Why do not both effects simply cancel each other? The two effects have different time constants (see box). The inhibition is a rapid process: after (partial) removal of an organizing region, the inhibition has to decay rapidly in order that regulation can occur. In contrast, competence should have a much longer time constant. It should remain almost unchanged at the time scale required for pattern regulation.

In the following simulation, competence is assumed to decline more strongly with increasing distance from the organizer. A distant fragment is no longer able to regenerate while fragments derived from regions closer to the original organizer have a better chance. This is a well-known property of many organizing regions in higher organisms.

In conclusion, not only the formation of a pattern is an important step. To make development reproducible and to suppress malformations it is also essential that the capability to form a particular pattern fades away at the correct later stage.
