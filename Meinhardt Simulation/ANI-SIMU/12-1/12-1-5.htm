Hydra, a living fossil, telling us about axes formation before bilaterality and trunk formation was invented during evolution







Ernst Heackel noticed already in 1874 that during development all higher organisms proceed through a cup-shaped tissue configuration he called ‘gastraea’, suggesting that this is close to the appearance of a common ancestor. Neglecting the tentacles, Hydra can be presumably regarded as a living fossil that maintains this cup-shaped structure when full-grown. The figure left below shows a drawing of an early developmental stage of Amphioxus, a lower vertebrate, in comparison with a Hydra, both consisting of an ectodermal and endodermal cell layer::


(left drawing from E. Haeckel. [1])

Thus, Hydra – or more general – the Cnidaria – are assumed to be close to the common ancestor before bilaterality was invented during evolution. However, the relation of the single axis in hydra and the two major body axes in higher organisms remained unclear for a long time.

The modern tools of molecular biology have provided an inroad. In 2000 Hobmayer et al. [2] demonstrated that in Hydra patterning molecules are involved that have been shown before to play a crucial role in organizer formation of higher organisms such as the frog: ß-catenin and WNT.  Nevertheless, it remained unclear whether the head-foot axis in Hydra is homologous to our anteroposterior axis. Alternatively, it has been argued that the high nerve cell density in the head of the Hydra corresponds to our neural side (spinal cord) such that the single axis of hydra would be homologues to our back-to-belly axis.

A comparison of reported expression patterns of homologous genes in hydra and in higher organisms at early stages of their development led to a very surprising result [3]. The bottom of the cup-shaped ancestor gave rise to the foot in Hydra and to the most anterior part of higher organisms, the forebrain and heart. Thus the Hydra foot and the forebrain of higher organisms have the same positional address. In contrast, the opening of the gastric column forms the most posterior structure. The so-called head in Hydra is, therefore, in reality not the front but the rear end and corresponds to the anus of higher organisms. The tentacle/hypostome border corresponds to the midbrain/hindbrain border :
 
 





Later in evolution the trunk has been sequentially intercalated into a narrow zone next to the gastric opening (yellow-red border in the drawing above). Thus, the common ancestor of hydra and higher organisms became almost entirely converted into our head and especially the brain. The trunk provided the precondition to form segments, limbs etc..

To proceed from the radial symmetric to a bilateral body plan, the crucial invention was the generation of a midline, i.e., the reference point for the mediolateral  patterning. In vertebrates, this is the notochord. It controls the formation of many other structures such as the spinal chord. Theoretical considerations revealed that the generation of a single long extended structure is a difficult task. What generates the asymmetry that the structure has a long extension in one dimension but only a small in the other? Early removal of midline structures in the chick embryo leads to their regeneration, showing that a the midline structure is maintained by a dynamic pattern-foming process. As shown above, saturation can lead to stripe-like patterns. However, when initiated by random fluctuations, the stripes bend and bifurcate. Straight stripes can be generated by the cooperation of a spot-producing and a stripe–producing system: the spot system triggers the stripe system while the stripe system repels the spot system [4]. In this way, the spot system elongates the stripe system):
 
 







This is what happens during midline formation in vertebrates: the organizer attracts cells of the ring-shaped margin that forms the opening of the cup-shaped organism (or the ectoderm/endoderm border; i.e., the marginal zone in the frog or the germ ring in the fish)  After being once close to the organizer, these cells become reprogrammed and are no longer attracted. They are left behind the moving organizer. In this way they form the midline, a process that  is based on the conversion of a ring- into a rod-like structure, including the most central structure, the notochord:

Thus, the notochord  - the reference point for our mediolateral organization - is generated by sequential elongation in the course of time. In contrast, the mechanism for midline formation in insects is totally different [3]. There, a dorsal organizer does not elongate but repels the midline, enforcing midline formation at the opposite ventral side. This occurs more or less synchronously over the whole AP-extension of, e.g., the fruit fly Drosophila  and not by a sequential elongation as  in vertebrates. Therefore, during evolution, starting from radial-symmetric animals, presumably two very the different modes to generate bilateral-symmetric organisms were invented.
 

 
Formation of a single stripe by local elongation: coupling of two activator inhibitor systems. The a, b system forms stripes due to saturation (sa>0).
