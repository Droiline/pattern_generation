Chains of induction

In the simulations on the preceding page, an organizing region was inducing a neighboring structure. The hypostome of a hydra, the cone-shaped structure around the opening of the gastric column, and the adjacent tentacles have been used as example. Experimental observations indicate that both the primary and the secondary structure are controlled by separate pattern-forming systems. Such a chain of inductions is not necessarily limited to two structures, since the induced structure can become again the inducing region for a subsequent structure. In the following it is shown that several activator-inhibitor systems can be linked to each other. Each structure has an activating influence on the formation of the adjacent structures. Locally, however, these structures exclude each other. Such systems are able to generate sequences of particular signals for differentiation in space. The resulting patterns show a very good size regulation over a wide range and are able to regenerate missing parts.

In the following simulation four coupled activator-inhibitor systems are assumed. A slight advantage of the `blue` system determines only the polarity of the resulting pattern. The sequence itself ('blue, green, red, brown) is fixed by strength of the mutual activations (see box). The fourfold subdivision of the field is stable over substantial growth.

With the removal of a portion of the field, some of the activated regions are removed too or become much reduced in size. The decreasing levels of the corresponding inhibitors enforce regulation. In conclusion, a mutual activation of several pattern-forming systems that are locally exclusive allows the generation of stable spatial sequence of structures. These show pattern regulation over a wide range of initial conditions, can adapt to different field size and are able to regulate after a perturbation.
