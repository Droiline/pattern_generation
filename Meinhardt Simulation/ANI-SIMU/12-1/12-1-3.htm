Formation of two structures close to each other:
hypostome and tentacle formation in Hydra as example




During development many structures emerge close to each other. Two different mechanisms are conceivable. Either the organizer is the source region of a substance that acts as morphogen, causing the activation of particular genes in certain concentration ranges and thus in a position-specific manner. Alternatively, the pattern forming system that gives rise to the organizer induces on longer range a second patterning system. Locally however, this second system is suppressed by the first. Then, the second system has to appear in the neighborhood of the first. In Hydra, at a certain distance from the opening of the gastric column, at the border of the so-called hypostome, ca. 5-6 tentacles appear. Tentacle formation is certainly based on a separate pattern forming system, otherwise only a ring-shaped structure but not separate elements along this ring-shaped zone would be formed.

If the spacing of the tentacles is governed by a scheme involving lateral inhibition, why only a single ring of tentacles is formed? Many experiments can be accounted for by the assumption that tentacles are under control of a tentacle-specific activator-inhibitor system that depends on a high source density (competence). Since the primary head system causes an increase in the source density in a wider area, the head system generates the precondition for tentacle initiation. Locally, however, the head signal suppresses tentacle formation. Therefore, tentacle formation (red) occurs next to the head. In contrast to the head system, the tentacle system has no influence on the competence. Since the competence for tentacle formation is highest in the region next to the hypostome, the tentacles are able to suppress the formation other tentacles in a larger surroundings. At even larger distances, the source density is assumed to be too low and thus the cells are incompetent for tentacle formation. Taking together, basic pattern formation in hydra requires at least three pattern forming systems: the hypostome (green), the tentacle system (red) and the foot system (black). All three systems are coupled to each other by the source density (or competence, blue). As described in detail in the book (page 212, Fig. 12.6) this model correctly describes a remarkable observation. With tentacle-specific antibodies, Bode et al. (1988) have shown that after head removal tentacle activation first reappears at the very tip of the gastric column (red in the drawing below).  First the patch becomes larger and the center de-activated. Somewhat later this ring-shaped activation shifts towards the final, slightly more basal position and disintegrates into separate patches. These local signals coincide with the actual tentacle formation.

The model reproduces this observation. The following simulations starts with the situation given above. Note that first the tentacle signal (red) appears at the apical end of the fragment. After the trigger of the hypostome signal (green) it becomes displaced:

According to the model, since tentacles are formed close together, the tentacle inhibitor need not diffuse very far. This is in contrast to the inhibitor for the primary organizing region that has to diffuse through the whole body column to prevent the formation of additional heads. Thus, after removal of the head and the tentacles, the tentacle inhibitor fades away more rapidly than the head inhibitor. The tentacle activator can reappear sooner than the head activator. Since initially no suppressing head activator is present, this happens at the highest possible source density (blue), i.e., at the apical end of the fragment. After the trigger of the primary head activation (green) at the same position, tentacle activation becomes shifted to the final location.

It was a prediction of the model that the system behaves differently in fragments derived from tissue closer to the foot. There, the competence for tentacle formation must be insufficient for a direct trigger. This is necessary since the tentacles appear close to each other. Therefore, that most of the gastric column is free of tentacles is not caused by a long-ranging inhibitory influence of the existing tentacles but result from the insufficient competence for tentacle formation. Thus, since a tentacle signal cannot be formed directly, the system has to wait until first the hypostome signal appears. Under its influence, the competence (blue) increases. If a certain level is reached, the signal for tentacle is triggered. Since the hypostome signal is then already present, the tentacle signal can only appear at their final positions:

As an approximation of the apical dome, the following simulations demonstrate formation of the tentacle signal in a two-dimensional field. The left simulation shows the situation with an initially high source density (blue, bottom profile): first a central tentacle signal (red) is formed that splits after the trigger of the head signal (green). This is the situation shown in the drawing above. In the simulation on the right, the source density is initially lower. First the hypostome signal appears that becomes subsequently surrounded by the ring of tentacles. A transient stage is a ring-shaped activation that disintegrates into several spots. There is never a central local activation as occurring during regeneration of an apical fragment. Recent expression studies with a Hydra homologue of the aristaless gene (Smith et al., Development 127, 4743; 2000) have provided further support of the model.

 
 

This model also accounts for the sequence of events in bud formation as distinct from that in head formation. Bud formation occurs in the course of growth in the body column. A new signal for head formation is generated in a region of low competence, a region that is incompetent for tentacle formation. Thus, tentacle formation is not allowed right away. It was expected, therefore, that first the hypostome signal is formed that rises subsequently the competence, creating in this way the precondition for the tentacle formation (corresponding to the simulation above right). The observations mentioned above have provided evidence for this prediction.
