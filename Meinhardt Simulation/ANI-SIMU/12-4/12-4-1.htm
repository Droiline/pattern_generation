Phyllotaxis: initiation of leaves along spirals



The regular initiation of new leaf primordia behind the tip of a growing shoot, the so-called phyllotaxis, seems to have nothing in common with any pattern on seashells. This impression is, however, misleading. On growing shoots as in shells, the new pattern elements are initiated in a narrow zone. The tip of the shoot, the meristem, contains undifferentiated cells that divide rapidly. Only cells just leaving this zone are able to generate new leaf primordia. Thus, the leaf-forming zone has the geometry of a narrow ring. As in shells, the final pattern of leaves or seedlings is a time record of what happens in the primordia-forming zone.

Many flowering plants display helical patterns. The same holds for the arrangement of seedlings. The fir cone shown below is an example. A comparison shows that in both systems the new elements - seedling or pigment spots - appear at regularly displaced positions.

As it has been shown above, signals can emerge at regular displaced positions if two antagonists are involved. In the simulation below, two inhibitors are assumed. One spreads rapidly and keeps the signal localized (red). The second one (green) has a longer time constant and spreads much less widely. It allows the formation of a further signal in the same region only after a certain time interval. Both effects together enforce the new signals to emerge at regularly shifted positions. In plants, the displacement between subsequent leaves is frequently close to 137.5°, the golden angle. This helical arrangement requires that the positioning of a new leaf is not only influenced by the last but also by the earlier leaves. This is easily understandable. Imagine that a first leaf exists. The second can appear at 180°. Only if the third leaf to be formed is exposed to some inhibitory influence from the first, it cannot be formed at the same circumferential position as the first and will become displaced. As discussed above for the shell patterns, this requires two antagonists with different properties. According to this model, the helical arrangement does not result from an inhibitory influence of the earlier leaves directly, but from the remnants of the long-lasting antagonist in the leaf-forming zone that a preceding leaf has left behind at this position.

If the diameter of the ring-shaped zone next to the meristem is small, there is space only for one maximum at the time. The simulation below shows the situation in the ring. The long-lasting inhibition is shown in green. A new activation arises always at a position where green is lowest. Although the simulation is initiated by random fluctuations only, the helical positioning is rapidly attained. Recent observations have shown that the plant hormone auxin is required as a prerequisite in leaf initiation (Reinhardt et al., 2000; Plant Cell 12, 507-518). Suppression of auxin transport suppresses leaf initiation, a situation that can be rescued by a local supply of auxin. This suggests that one of the expected antagonistic reactions results from a depletion of auxin in a wider surrounding. The figure below shows snapshots of a corresponding simulation. Auxin is shown in gray, the long lasting inhibition in red. The upper row shows the coming and going of a signal to initiate a primordium. A new primordium appears when auxin concentration is high enough and the inhibitor low enough. The primordium remains localized due to a depletion of auxin in a wider region. The blue arrow points to the site of the least concentration of the long-lasting inhibitor, foreshadowing the position where the next leaf primordia will arise.
 
 

The bottom row shows the initiation of the two subsequent primordia at displaced positions. In the simulation it is assumed that the spread of auxin is accomplished by diffusion. A more realistic model has to include its active transport.

The displacement by the golden angle is a stable configuration attained in a wider range of parameters and interactions. The simulation below is based on the same interaction as used above. As used in many botanical textbooks, the position of the activated regions are plotted on circles with shrinking radius, the most recent primordia are close to the center. The angle drawn has 137,5°. As the simulation shows, the angular separation of two successive primordia is close to the golden angle.

The notion of 137.5° may suggest a very high precision. However, as shown in the two figures at right, the natural patterns also show some variation.

Also common are patterns in which new leaves either appear at alternating opposing positions (distichous patterns) or in a pair-wise arrangement in which each subsequent pair is rotated by 90° (decussate pattern). The generation of paired peaks has been discussed already above in connection with the non-random patterning of isolated chemotactic cells. They occur if the size of the leaf-forming zone is sufficiently large such that two peak can be formed simultaneously and if the diffusion of the long-lasting antagonist is high enough such that the new maxima appear precisely in the gaps of the previous maxima:

Whether auxin is involved as a long-ranging signal is an open question. From the modeling, the use of a real inhibitor as antagonist (as used in the simulations at the top of this page, Eq. 5.4) leads in the modeling usually to more robust patterning. This has its origin from the tendency of maxima generated by a depletion mechanism to broaden and to split.

According to this model, the spacing between leaves depends not only on the inhibition of the already established leaf primordia on the initiation of the next. Proposed is that two different inhibitions are involved: one around the circumference and one that determines when new primordia can be formed again. The necessity of such a separation is especially evident in plants with a whorl-like arrangement of leaves that are separated by leaf-free zones:

There are certainly other elements that have to be included in future models, such as the real geometry of the dome-shaped tip of the shoot, the rapid differentiation of the upper and lower leaf surface and the feedback of leaf primordia onto the meristem.
