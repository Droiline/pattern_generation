Traveling gaps

After tongue initiation, the next dark pigmentation is usually involved to trigger the traveling wave that leads to closure of the gap. Occasionally, however, several oscillations are required before a traveling wave (oblique line) is formed. The arrows mark such a situation on the shell below. .

Usually, the size of the tongue starts to shrink only after the traveling wave has been formed. This indicates that the pigmentation system is needed to enlarge the zone in which the precondition is in the ON-state. This view is supported by patterns that display tongues that enlarge over time. This crumbling away takes place only in regions where the tongues are not bordered by a traveling wave. On some shells gaps are visible that enlarge on one side and shrink on the other. Therefore, the tongues move in a wave-like manner.

  

This phenomenon is reproduced in the simulation:

The following simulation shows a moving gap in a movie-like manner. It is the same simulation as shown above right in the space-time plot. The retraction of precondition pattern (green) after the formation of an initially small gap is clearly visible. After the pigmentation system is triggered, the gap slowly closes. The elevated production of the "poison" in the center (curve on top of simulation above left) leads to the preferential gap formation in the center.

The moving gaps shed light on the interplay between the two systems. On its own, the precondition system may have a tendency to retract. In contrast, the pigmentation system has a tendency to move into the gap since more substrate is available there. Gap formation and its closure is thus a fight between the retraction of the precondition system and the spread of the oscillating pigmentation system, pulling the precondition system with it by its refresh function. Accordingly, the probability of traveling gaps increases with a decrease in the rate of substrate diffusion, since the attraction of pigmentation system towards the gap is lower. As in the real pattern, only the lower side of such an oblique gap is bordered by a dark oblique pigmentation line. It is the side in which the precondition system becomes restored and this requires the help of the pigment system. The movement of the gap terminates when the pigmentation system is also triggered at the crumbling side, causing a bilateral spread into the gap and thus its closure.

On the shell shown above, the gaps are arranged in a fairly regular way. TMost of them have a nearly circular shape. This is, however, not trivial. In the upper part of the circle, a widening of the gap occurs. This depends on the speed of retraction of the precondition pattern. In contrast, the lower half of the circle in which the gap becomes narrower and ultimately closed, depends on the waves that pull the precondition pattern behind. For the circular appearance, both speeds must be roughly the same. The heavy dark line at the lower half of the circle indicates that the activation in these traveling waves occurs with some overshoot (for another shell with circular gaps click here).
