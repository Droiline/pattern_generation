The restriction of parallel lines into bands

The restriction of the parallel lines into bands is a characteristic feature of many Clithon shells. Oblique lines frequently connected these bands. Obviously some stable pattern exists that allows synchronous oscillations only in particular regions.

One way to model this is to assume a higher production rate of the poison (parameter be in equation given below). Under this condition, the activation of the precondition pattern is too short to allow several oscillations. Isolated oblique lines (traveling waves) are still possible in such regions. They have their origin in one of the parallel lines. Below the top part of the simulation above is shown in an animated form.



In Clithon, isolated oblique lines usually don’t branch. This is in strong contrast, for instance, to the pattern on Conus textile where, outside of the parallel lines, branching is a characteristic feature. According to the model, the oblique lines in Clithon result from a wave-like spread of both the precondition and the pigmentation system. In contrast, in patterns with branching, the oblique lines are formed by one system only. The occasional triggering of the second system causes the formation of a branch (compare the black-green line in simulation above with the green dots along black lines in the branching pattern). In the present patterning system, when the precondition remains active for a sufficiently long period, the result is not a branch but two or more successive parallel lines.
