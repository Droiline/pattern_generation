The spacing of the tongues


In the following simulations, the precondition pattern is plotted in green. Oscillations can occur only if the precondition is in the ON-state. The rapid spread of the pigment system leads to the synchronization of the oscillation. Nevertheless it takes time to close a gap since this requires the activation of the precondition system, which is a time-consuming process. Thus, this model reconciles the rapid spread as required for the synchronization and the apparent slow spread for filling the gaps.

What spaces tongue formation? On some shells the tongues are arranged in a scattered way, on others they appear more regular at staggered positions. The latter suggest that whenever a tongue has been formed, some memory exists in the corresponding region that delays the formation of the next tongue. During tongue formation a substance may be produced that stabilizes subsequent oscillations. Alternatively, either the oscillating or background system may produce a poison that eventually leads to the breakdown of the precondition system before the next refresh occurs. A decrease in oscillation frequency would have the same effect. Occasionally a somewhat larger spacing between the parallel lines is visible at the onset of tongue formation, arguing in favor of the latter possibility (see shell on the previous page).  In the simulations shown above it is assumed that the precondition system produces an additional long-lasting inhibitor. Its accumulation makes in the course an abrupt breakdown of the precondition system more likely. The existence of a regulatory mechanism can be deduced from the shell shown below. The parallel lines follow so densely on each other that pigmentation appears almost homogeneous. Under this condition an enhanced pigmentation is visible. It has the appearance of a long faint shadow under each (almost ring-shaped) gap. Further gaps form only at the side or after the fading of the shadows.

The probability of triggering a gap can be modified by either by a change of the production rate or of the efficiency of the poison (se in the equation below). A decrease in the efficiency leads to fewer tongues (left), and vice versa (right).

Although many essential elements are correctly reproduced, some problems in the simulations remain.  For instance, it was necessary to assume some diffusion of the substrate (b in the equation above) to make the gap attractive for the pigment reaction. This has unwanted consequences. At the moment the gaps are ready for closing, the waves become weaker due to the lateral inhibition resulting from the substrate diffusion. A comparison with the real pattern shows that this is not correct. The staggered character of the tongues is also less pronounced in the simulations compared with the natural patterns. The natural patterns suggest that the disappearance of the precondition pattern is abrupt. Therefore, in a model the accumulating signal should have no influence on the pattern until the actual breakdown takes place abruptly. How can such an accumulation proceed for long without any overt manifestation? The same problem was accounted in the simulation for Conus marmoreus. However, an active extinguishing reaction as used for Conus marmoreus is unlikely to exist for the Clithon patterns.  Finding a solution for this problem was a crucial constraint in designing the interaction.
